Chapter 2: The Indian Act

“The great aim of our legislation has been to do away with the tribal system and assimilate the Indian people in all respects with the other inhabitants of the Dominion as speedily as they are fit to change.”

Sir John A. Macdonald, 1887

The Indian Act was introduced in 1876. The Government of Canada used this legislation to administer Indian status, local First Nations governments and the management of reserve land and communal monies. Today, the Indian Act applies only to First Nations and Status Indians. It does not apply to Non-Status Indians, Métis or Inuit.

As Sir John A. MacDonald tells us, the Indian Act was never intended to protect the culture, language or beliefs of “Indian people.” In effect, it has enabled human rights violations, trauma and social, cultural and political disruption for generations of First Nations peoples.
