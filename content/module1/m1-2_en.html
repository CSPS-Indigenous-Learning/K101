Who are First Nations peoples?

Culture is an important part of a person’s identity. In course 2, you learned that many Indigenous peoples lost much of their culture through colonization. Today, First Nations Elders, youth and communities are working together to revive their cultures and languages.

Language and culture

Language is more than a means of communication. It is a vital part of culture—a way to perceive and understand the world. As a cultural vehicle, language:

  • influences social and political systems and spiritual practices
  • interprets the environment and reflects a unique world view
  • embodies the intellectual wealth of generations of people who have used and enriched it
  • carries centuries of stories from the past and understandings of the world

Self-reflection: Think of your own first language. What does being able to speak it freely mean to you? How does your language allow you to access services such as health care or education?

Indigenous languages

Indigenous languages are numerous and diverse, reflecting the rich variety of First Nations cultures and identities. Did you know that there are over 60 ways to say “hello” in the Indigenous languages spoken in Canada today? These languages belong to 11 language families—10 used by First Nations, 1 used by the Inuit.

Indigenous language families include:

  • Athabaskan (Na-Dene)
  • Algonquian (Algic)
  • Iroquoian
  • Siouan
  • Kutenai
  • Salishan
  • Tsimshianic
  • Haida
  • Wakashan
  • Tlingit
  • Inuktitut (Inuit)

$90 million was allocated to saving and protecting Indigenous languages and cultures in 2017.

There are many distinct languages and dialects within these language families, each reflecting a unique history and culture. To learn more about where the various Indigenous languages are spoken in Canada, explore Canadian Geographic’s Indigenous Languages Map

Original languages endangered

Many of our country’s original languages are in danger of disappearing. The UNESCO Atlas of the World’s Languages in Danger shows that three quarters of Canada’s Indigenous languages are “definitely,” “severely” or “critically” endangered. The rest are classified as “vulnerable.”

Today, only Inuktitut, Cree and Ojibwa are thought to have enough speakers to continue indefinitely.

Recognizing the role played by the Government of Canada in the deterioration of Indigenous languages, Call to Action #14 (iii) of the Truth and Reconciliation Commission states, “The federal government has a responsibility to provide sufficient funds for Aboriginal-language revitalization and preservation.”

The Government of Canada can support Indigenous communities in their work to revitalize many original languages:

  • In June 2017, the federal government committed to enact First Nations, Inuit and Métis languages legislation
  • It established a collaborative process with the Assembly of First Nations, Inuit Tapiriit Kanatami and the Métis Nation “to engage Indigenous peoples, including language experts, language keepers and academics to inform what should be considered in the legislation”

With legislation, strong policies and stable funding, the Government of Canada can make a difference in supporting Indigenous language revitalization.
